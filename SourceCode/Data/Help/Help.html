Hebrew Bible Student - Help

1 General Information about Old Testament Student (OTS)

The aim of Old Testament Student (OTS) is to provide you with a version of the Old Testament in its original languages, with some tools for exploring it on your own computer, This means that you can use it even when the internet is not available. It also means that you can work at your computer's speed and not that of your internet connection. (Please see the final section, Caveats and Compliments, for a brief explanation of sources and acknowledgements.)

We'll introduce you to the different parts of the application in detail later but let's get an overview now of what Bible Reader offers. It provides
  • The text of the Old Testament as provided by Biblia Hebraica Stuttgartensia, displayed for a selected chapter;
  • Alternatively, you can read Rhalfs edition of the Septuagint (the Greek translation of the Old Testament, made around 200 BC);
  • A parse window: simply by clicking and "Analysing" a word, you can get a grammatical analysis of that word;
  • At the same time, it will also try to give you details of how the word was used, as provided by the BDB (Brown, Driver and Briggs) lexicon for the Hebrew and Aramaic or Liddell & Scott for the Greek;
  • A search facility allows you to get a list of all the verse where the word is used, together with some additional sophisitcation in search criteria;
  • A notes area: a feature that allows you to create, save and restore notes for each verse.
So, let's look at the application in more detail.

  • when looking at the LXX, this area includes information that may help decode some of the abbreviations used in the Liddell & Scott lexicon entries.



3 The main Text Area

3.1 The Basics

It's pretty obvious what happens here:


You can change the book you are reading by selecting from the list in the Book list, in the Orientation Panel. Similarly, you can change the chapter alone by selecting from the Chapter list. Note that, if you change the book, it will automatically reset to the first chapter of that book.

3.2 Some Additional Options

If you right click anywhere on the text area, a small menu will pop up:


The first four application options (after Share) open a mini-window known as a "dialog" (spelt the American way):

Whether you are copying a single word, a verse or the whole chapter, you will get the same dialog of options. This will allow you to specify:

  • whether the target destination of the copy is memory or the relevant Notes page;
  • whether to include the reference in the copy or not;

You can also check the box to ensure that the options you select are remembered. This will mean that future copies will automatically use the same options. Note, however, that

3.2.1 Copy Current Word, Verse or Chapter to ...

This will do what the image tells you.

If you select Memory, this will copy the currently selected word into memory (technically, into the "Pasteboard"). This means that you can paste it into another application, such as a Word Processor. (If you're not sure how to paste into your application, check its help facility, look on Google or ask a friend.) The option to omit accents allows you to copy a clearer version of the text, not hampered by the complex accenting system of Hebrew or Greek.

If you select Notes, it will copy the word to the notes area for the current verse at the point where your cursor is currently positioned.

When you right click to get the Context Menu, make sure that the mouse is over the specific result and word that you are interested in.

Be aware that the concept of a word in most European languages needs a degree of refinement in Hebrew.  We normally think of a word as a sequence of letters (or some kind of more precise equivalent) separated from others by spaces and/or grammatical symbols.  It's not that easy in Hebrew.  Consider Genesis 25:2 and the "word":

וְאֶת־יָקְשָׁ֔ן

There are two obvious components to this word: the "main" word, יָקְשָׁן, a masculine noun which is, in fact, a man's name, and the component to the right of the mappeq.  But that's not all, this second element can be sub-divided into the preposition, אֶת, and the conjunction, וְ.  Are these individual words or something else that only become words when used in certain ways?  Well, for better or worse, they are divided into word-like units in BHS, so be careful where you place your mouse when you open this pop-up menu.

3.2.2 Copy Selected Text to ...

You can select a small amount of text by using a click and drag technique: click the left mouse of the button, as normal, at the place where you want to selection to start but hold the button down rather than letting go; then, while holding the button down, drag the mouse until it is over the place where you want the selection to finish; then let go. Once you have done this, you can right click anywhere in the text area and select the menu option to copy the selected text. Note that you can also perform this task by double-clicking, which will select a whole word, and then dragging to select additional words.  (Bear in mind, though, the comments earlier on what is a word.)

Note that you will be presented with the same dialog and the same options as other copy actions.

3.2.3 Reset Copy Options

If you have not chosen to remember the settings for any of the copy options, this menu option will not be available, since you don't need it.  Assuming that you have remembered one or more copy settings, clicking on this menu option will raise another dialog:

If you have chosen to remember copy details for one or more types of copy options, then this is the way you can get the application to prompt you once again. This means that you will be able to select different options.

Note that each copy option is treated separately, including treating the BHS and LXX separately.

Our text always presents the Qere form but, by default, displays the word in red. If you right click on one of these words, an extra option will appear in the context menu:

4 Analysis

This is a convenient description for the action of creating:
  1. Parse details, and
  2. Lexical information for the selected word.

If you right click on a word in the text area, you will see a small context menu pop up.  Then select Analyse Word option from the resulting pop-up menu

to obtain parsing details and lexical information for that word. You will see the parsing information on the right hand, top pane immediately:



We have selected the word to be analysed so that you can compare the final result with where we started.  You do not need to do this to perform an analysis: a simple right click will suffice.

Note that our earlier comments on the nature of words applies here.  We've deliberately chosen a word that contains a main lexical item, a preposition and the standard conjunction.  Each are treated seperately by OTS.

We need to provide a caveat that applies to all elements of OTS.  This parsing information has been provided in the source data and we cannot guarantee its accuracy.  It may provide you a clue when you find a word difficult but this tool does not absolve you from the need of understanding your Hebrew (or Aramaic) grammar and reflecting critically on the text you are reading.  We are not responsible for you failing you PhD because you accepted our parse or lexical information too readily.

To see the dictionary definition, click on the Lexical Information tab:


These are actually "candidate entries" in BDB for the selected word.  OTS uses a somewhat tortuous method to identify the potential BDB entry which requires a number of caveats.
  1. They are based, to a large extent, on entries in Strong's Concordance which is generally considered to be somewhat unreliable in academic circles;
  2. The selection method often results in multiple possible entries, so all have been given;
  3. We may simply have got the link wrong.

The upshot of these caveats is that using OTS does not absolve you from the need to exercise your own critical facilities.  Where there are multiple entries, you will need to select the one you think is correct.  In all cases, you will also need to assure yourself that this meaning is correct.  (Even if you use BDB in the traditional way, or are a devotee of Koehler & Baumgartner, the need to be critical in your use of their studies also applies.)

Note that parse details and lexical information will remain available until you request a new analysis and that changing tabs does not erase data.

Analysing Greek words occurs in much the same way (except, of course, a different lexicon is used). It should be noted that the source data sometimes identified a base form for some words that were not considered as base forms by Liddell and Scott. A good example of this is words that are only found as deponents in the LXX and, so, the base form would be -ομαι. If the active form is found in classical or wider Greek, Liddell & Scott may have entered this under its active form (e.g. -ω). We have tried to build a number of strategies into the search process but we cannot guarantee that all words will be identified.

6. Searching

If you click on the Search tab in the lower right pane, you will find a fairly busy pane which allows you to configure a search:


This will take quite a bit of unpacking and some time to get used to, but it is to be hoped that this will be an important part of OTS.

Obviously, the word to search for must be entered into the first textbox: Search for the word. The question is: How do you type a Hebrew word into the box?

Probably the most useful mechanism requires a return to the text area. If you right click on a word in the text area, a context menu option allows you to copy that word into this box:


If you select this menu option it will insert the selected word into the box.



So, with the word in the textbox, simply click on the Perform Search button and OTS will look for all the occurrences in the OT:


Notice the scroll bar to the right of the search results. This means that you can click on the light grey area below the "thumb" and scroll down a page for more results. Alternatively, you can "drag" the darker grey thumb (the small square) and drag down as much or as little as you want.

Notice also the message at the bottom of the results. This was created while it was performing the search and is a report on progress. For searches involving frequently used words, the progress of the search is made visible here.  Be patient: the application is scanning through a lot of text and can take a little while.

a) Specific books

If you click on one of the books in the listbox, it will be highlighted (or "selected"). If you cmd+click on a second book, that too will be selected and the first one will stay highlighted. You can scroll through the list of books and select specific ones:


If we now click on the Remove Selected Books, OTS will remove those books from the search criteria:


Look carefully and you will see that the three selected books are no longer in the list. Incidentally, if you clicked on a book in error and it was selected, simply cmd+click on it again and it will be un-selected,

6.2 Excluding and including whole collections of books

If I want to exclude whole groups of books, I can use the check boxes to the right of the book list. Imagine, for example, that I don't want to search the books of Moses. Then, I can simply click on the checkbox, Pentateuch and they will disappear from the list:

                             


Note that the list of available books is different in the LXX. In particular, a number of additional books are included (which are generally considered as pseudo-canonical or apocryphal by Protestants). Some canonical books also occur under different names and the order of chapters and verses may also vary.

You may have noticed the two buttons above the book list: one says Exclude and the other says Include. These are "radio buttons": clicking on one un-clicks the other; they are an either/or choice. If you select Include (the listed books), then any books showing in the listbox will be included in the final search. On the other hand, if you select Exclude (the listed books), then only the books not in the listbox will be included in the search . At any time, if you click on Reinstate Books to default settings, all the books will reappear.  So, using these simple selection tools, you can quickly and easily narrow your search criteria.

6.3 A second way of entering your search word

In general, I have found the method described above as the most common method by far. However, there is a second way. If you look on the bottom left of the application window (in the yellow area), you will see the button: Show Keyboard.  Click on this button and a virtual keyboard will be revealed:


As you type using this virtual keyboard, the result of your typing will appear in the textbox near the bottom right of the area.  When you are satisfied that you want to use the word that you have typed, select the relevant radio button above the textbox and it will determine where any word you type will go. The main benefit of this keyboard is that you can easily enter Hebrew text into your notes. However, you can also use this to populate your search option by selecting the Primary Search Word radio button option.

Of course, you "type" using the mouse. These do not respond to keyboard presses. To get the vowels or sin/shin dot, click on the relevant consonant first, then on the special character. If the addition is not allowed, it will simply be ignored.

You may need to experiment with this option a little. You will also need to be careful that you have typed the word exactly.

We have shown the Hebrew/Aramaic keyboard but a similarly provided Greek virtual keyboard is provided when in the LXX section.

This will then modify the search area:


When you click on Perform Search you will now search for all occurrences of the primary word that occur within n words of the secondary word:


You will need to be very patient when using this option: the search process is a lot more complex and can take quite some time.

(Note, for example, the first result, where the match spans two verses.  It is not the most intuitive presentation and you will need to look carefully to recognise that the two search words are actually almost next to each other.)

You can vary the strictness or slackness of criteria by varying the "distance" of the two words, up to a separation of 10 words. It will be for you to judge how far the two words can be apart before their mutual occurrence becomes coincidental rather than significant.  Remember also that "word" means something a little different: two main words "next to each other" might actually be three or four words apart.  If you are searching for two related words and one is much less frequent generally than the other, we recommend that you make the less frequent word the Primary Word.  That should speed the search somewhat.

6.5 Different types of search

You will have noticed that, beneath the primary (and secondary) word, there is an option to select one of three strategies for matching words:

  • Matching BDB references;
  • Matching the exact form of the selected word (but ignoring accents);
  • Matching the exact consonants of the selected word.

These will need a little additional explanation.

6.5.1 Matching BDB references

We have already explained that the source data provides us with one or more BDB references for most words, which has allowed us to identify potential meaning (lexical entries) for words. This option will find other occurrences of words with one or more of the same BDB references. Note that, since some of these references are tangential links, it may create "matches" that are somewhat tenuous. Equally, it may create matches that you might not expect and they could be quite fruitful. As with much of the application, this is an aid and doesn't replace the need for you to exercise reason and caution.

6.5.2 Matching the exact form

This is the strictest of the three options. This means that a search for יֹּאמֶר (for example, from Gen 926) will not match יֵאָמַר in Gen 109, because the two word forms have different vowels.

6.5.3 Matching consonants but not vowels (or accents)

This is a kind of compromise between the very loose match criteria of the first option and the tight criteria of the second. In this case, the two forms of the verb would be matched but a second person form would still be excluded.

6.5.4 Greek Matching options

In Greek searches, the equivalent function is a little simpler. Here, you can either

  • match on base (lexical) forms - referred to as "roots" but they are not normally true roots, or
  • identical matches (although accents are ignored).

6.6 Using the Search Results

These search results are primarily for your information. They form a kind of concordance. However, you can also make use of them. Right click on any specific result and you will see the following Context Menu:


6.6.1 Copy Results or Single Result to ...
You will see that this will allow you to copy either all the search results or just the specifically selected result to either memory or to the currently active notes area.  These two destinations work in much the same way as §3.2 above.  Remember that, if you copy it to the notes area, it will be active in the note for the currently selected verse.
6.6.2 Change Text to Selected Chapter
Again, you need to be careful where you click for this option. It will change the book and chapter in the left (Text) pane to that of the result. So, for example, if you select this option on the result for Genesis 18:10, it will change the text presented in the left pane to Genesis 18.

7. Notes

This is the first tab in the area at the bottom right of the window.  This is an area where you can simply make notes about a given verse. Each verse has its own notes area and, when you change to a different verse (whether in the same chapter or elsewhere), the note will disappear and, if the new destination already has a note, the new note will appear. So, if you change something and the note disappears, don't be too worried: moving back to the original verse should restore the note as well.

We will learn more about notes in section 9.2 below.

Main LXX Text Area:

Grammatical Description Area:

  • Background colour;
  • Size, font and style of the parse text and headings independantly;

Word Meaning Area (i.e. Lexicon):

  • Background colour;
  • Size, font and style of the main text and headings independantly;
  • Colour of both of the text types independantly;
  • For Hebrew and Aramaic meanings, Hebrew/Aramaic text can also be varied in a similar way to the others.

Search Results Areas:

  • Background colour;
  • Size, font and style of the reference text, general text, words that constituted the primary search criteria and secondary search criteria independantly;
  • Colour of all of the above text types independantly

Notes Areas:

  • Background colour;
  • Size, font and style of the text;
  • Colour of the text

Why change colours and text

This might seem a little unnecessary but some people find black-on-white uncomfortable and changing colours may make it easy to use the application for long periods of time. (There is some suggestion that symptoms of dyslexia and those on the Aspergers spectrum are eased when changing colours.)

In addition, here are some suggested reasons why some might find it useful:

  • As a person of more mature years, the ability to increase text size might have significant benefits;
  • Changing colours or replacing colour differentiation by, e.g., use of italics and bold might help people who have difficulty in distinguishing certain colours;
  • Having different background colours might help emphasise the different areas of the application.

Remember, none of the changes are actually activated until you click on the OK button.

10 Additional Information about the Main Window

10.1 Getting the Window the way you want it

You can resize the window by dragging the window edges or corners in any desired direction. We will assume that you are familiar with resizing windows (and click-and-drag actions, in particular) and, if you are not, we recommend that you read a book on using your operating system.  The application will remember any changes you make to the size and position of the main window and will open it to look the same the next time you use OTS.  In order to make sure this is the case, we suggest that you always use the Close button.

The window is divided into two halves and, between the main text area and the collection of small panes on the right hand side, there is a Splitter Bar. Slowly move your mouse from the main text area across to the right and, as you cross the divider, the cursor shape should change. At the point the curser has changed you can click and drag the bar with your mouse. The benefit of this change is that you can maximise the area in which you have main interest.  The position of the splitter bar will also be remembered between sessions.

11 Adjusting the right-hand Pane

The one control on the top tool bar that we haven't discussed so far can also be found on the main menu, under View:


The purpose of this feature is to enable you to easily manage the information in the various sections on the right-hand side of the application. Sometimes you may want to concentrate on information in the top panel (e.g. Search Results) and at other times you may want to focus on the lower panel (e.g. if creating a complex note). This feature will enable you to resize these sub-panels in a controlled fashion.

There are actually three options available.  These are:

11.1 Maximise Top Right Pane

In other words, reduce the bottom panel to an absolute minimum and allow you to focus on the top panel. To use this option, simply click on the down arrow beside the Resize control and select the Minimise Bottom option:

11.2 Maximise Bottom Right Pane

This has a similar effect but works in the other direction.

11.3 Equalise Both Right Panes

This will restore the dividing bar between the two sub-panels to the mid-point vertically. This means that both the top and bottom panel on the right hand side will be roughly the same size.

You are still able to resize these panels manually by clicking on the Splitter Bar and dragging it into the desired position.

It should be noted, however, that only two of these options are visible at any time.  The third, hidden option is the one that would leave you as you are.  So, only options that change the state of the right hand areas are visible in the View menu options.

12. Caveats and Compliments

The text of the OT used in this application is the Biblia Hebraica Stuttgartensia (1977), © Deutsche Bibelstiftung Stuttgart.  To purchase definitve copies in either paper or electronic format, see Hebräisch | Wissenschaftliche Bibelausgaben | Bibelstudium | Shop | Die-Bibel.de (die-bibel.de).  Note that you will also find a growing number of books of the Bible available in the Biblia Hebraica Quinta (BHQ) format:These are effectively the new version of BHS but, since they are currently being sold in individual books, are quite expensive.

Note that Biblia Hebraica Stuttgartensia: A Reader's Edition is also available.  This provides a Hebrew-English dictionary and parses all but the more frequent words.

The text used in BHS is based on the work of Eliran Wong, which is apparently the basis of the Open Hebrew Bible (see eliranwong (Eliran Wong) · GitHub).    For more about Pastor Eliran Wong, see https://www.ecclondon.com/en/eliran-wong/.

This text was apparently derived from that of ETCBC.  This appears to be the work of the Faculty of Theology of VU University Amsterdam and is licensed under a Creative Commons Attribution-NonCommercial 4.0 International License.  That means that you can use the text for non-commercial purposes.

As has been said above, the lexicon used is that of the BDB (Brown, Driver and Briggs; Oxford, probably the 1936 version).    This text is in the public domain and source files have been released under the Creative Commons Attribution 4.0 International license.  The sources used have also included references to Strong's Concordance, which seem to reflect the work of David Troidl and Daniel Owens.

The version of the LXX used seems to have originated from CCAT (which, in turn, is based on the Rahlfs text) but some modifications were facilitated from a member of a Classical Greek forum and, sadly, we did not note his name or details at the time and are unable to make adequate acknowledgements. It is with sorrow that we admit this.

Because the path to the final versions of both BHS and LXX have been long and convoluted, there is every probability that the data contains errors. We apologise for this and counsel caution in using the application: where conclusions are significant, check your facts carefully. But, then, you should do this anyway! Despite these words of caution, we think the benefits of using the software still outweigh the problems.

As is obvious from the above, the development of easily available Hebrew and Greek texts of a good quality have involved the labours of many and we wish to express profound thanks for all who have given time and effort to make the scriptures available to all in their original (or significant) language.
